Syntax trees of the Bible

A brief overview of Syntactic Bible Tree app.

This tutorial will guide you through the different language functionalities used in the Syntactic Bible Tree app.

Access the website here.

Syntax trees are visual representations of the grammatical structure of sentences. These trees break down sentences into their constituent parts, such as clauses, phrases, and words, and show how these components are organized and relate to each other within the sentence. They assist you in developing your language education in the bible as well as being able to dissect more complex sentences in the bible.

Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Getting Started

When you open the website, you should see you should see a navigation bar that looks like this

The "Book" option lets you select a verse in the bible to be viewed. The "Setting" option lets you customize the output.

This will be explained further in the next step. Go ahead and select a verse from the "Book" option. You can only select one verse at a time.

Step 2: Adjusting Settings

Once you have selected a verse, you should see a tree structure like this pop up.

If you chose a verse from The Old Testament you should see a tree with Hebrew at the bottom. If you chose a New Testament verse, it should be in Greek. The Hebrew trees are verse-based,while the Greek trees are sentence-based, where a tree can cover part of a verse or span more than one verse.

When you hover over any of the blue text of the tree, you should see a small yellow box pop over the text. It looks like this:

This popover box a shows the Strong number of the word and its morphological properties such as part of speech, person, number, gender, tense, voice, etc. Instructions to change the content of the popover can be found below.

When you click the "Setting" button, you will see a popup window that looks like this

The Old Testament option determines how you want the Hebrew text of the Old Testament to be displayed. Since Hebrew is read from right to left, the default display is right-to-left, but you are given the option of reading it from left to right.

The Glossary Language option lets you pick which language is displayed below the original text. You can choose English, Chinese, or both. An example of this can be seen in the Tree Example above, where the English text is below the Hebrew text.

The Popover Language options lets you choose what language the popover box displays. Unlike Glossary Language you can only choose one language at a time.

Step 3: Understanding the Tree

Now that you have loaded the tree and understand the settings, you can begin analyzing the tree structure. Below is an explanation of the tree structure. It is applicable to both Greek and Hebrew.

For the purpose of this tutorial, here is another example tree, this time in Greek.

Root Label ("Verse"): Represents the entire sentence or verse being analyzed.

Tree Structure: the branches show how the sentence is broken down into smaller parts, called constituents.

Constituent Labels:

  • CL: a syntactic unit usually containing a subject and a predicate.
  • SUBJ: Subject, the doer of an action or the topic of a sentence.
  • V: Verb, indicating an event/action or state of being.
  • PP: Prepositional Phrase, a modifying phrase consisting of a preposition and it's object.
  • OBJ: Object, the receiver/goal of the action of the verb.
  • np: Noun Phrase, a group of words that act together as a noun.

Parts of Speech:

  • noun: Naming a word for a person, place, thing, or idea.
  • det:Determiner, a word that introduces a noun (like "the")
  • om: Object marker, a Hebrew-specific word that marks the direct object of a sentence.
  • conj: Conjunction, a word that connects clauses or sentences or coordinates words in the same clause. (like"and)
  • verb: A word that represents an event/action or state of being
  • prep: Preposition, a word used to link nouns,pronouns, or phrases to other words within a sentence.

Direction of Analysis: The tree structure typically moves from top (the whole sentence) to bottom (individual words or morphemes), demonstrating how each part of the sentence relates to the whole.
